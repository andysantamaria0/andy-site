Foundation
The Existing Vialoure DNA
What we keep, what we evolve
Vialoure has a beautiful foundation — the ornate symbol mark with its decorative notched border, the striking logotype, the signature notch, and a palette of Charcoal (#2F2F2F), Ivory (#F4F4ED), Hide (#DFB288), Royal Blue (#4A35D7), and Coral (#FF7D73). The Karla + Tiempos pairing brings contrast and warmth. Each direction below preserves this core while pushing into territory inspired by Italian Art Deco, the French Riviera, and golden-age travel.
Core Elements — Retained

The Symbol

The ornate V within the scalloped border frame. Its decorative corners evoke vintage luggage labels and hotel crests — perfect for where we’re headed.

The Signature Notch

The peaked triangular element — a tent, a mountain, an upward journey. Used as a divider, accent, and wayfinding device. Each system adapts its application.

The Logotype

The bespoke Vialoure wordmark with distinctive high-contrast serifs and open counters. Luxury editorial — a natural fit for vintage travel.

The Name

“Vialoure” — via + allure, a golden path. Each system should make you feel like you’re holding a first-class ticket to somewhere extraordinary.

System I
Riviera d’Oro
“The golden hour never ends on this coast”
Imagine Fitzgerald and Zelda pulling up to the Hôtel du Cap in a cream-colored Bugatti. This system leans into the sun-warmed Mediterranean — terracotta, saffron, aged parchment — grounded by deep navy. Soft, inviting, nostalgic glow like looking through a villa window in Positano. Typography pairs elegant Didone display with clean geometric sans. Generous radii, warm shadows, subtle paper textures.

Design Principles

Warm Over Cool

Every surface feels sun-touched. Shadows use warm amber, never pure grey. Backgrounds carry subtle warmth — aged parchment, Tuscan plaster.

Editorial Grace

Magazine conventions — generous whitespace, asymmetric grids, type that breathes. Like it was art-directed for Condé Nast Traveler circa 1962.

Subtle Texture

Light paper grain on backgrounds. Barely-there noise on cards. Think fine linen envelope, not concrete wall.

The Golden Ratio

Radii 12–20px. Soft warm shadows. Cards feel like photographs on a table. The notch as dividers and section markers.

System II
Manifesto
“Bold geometry, a splash of campari, and the courage to go”
Inspired by Bo Lundberg’s Rome 73 and Turin 76 posters, Italian Futurism, and bold flat graphics of 1930s–70s travel posters. The most graphic and energetic — confident shapes, high-contrast color blocking, typography that demands attention. Concentric circles, strong diagonals, flat color fields. The app becomes a poster you can interact with.

Design Principles

Poster Logic

Every screen should work as a poster. Strong hierarchy, bold color blocking, type that reads across the room. If you squint and it still looks good, you’ve nailed it.

Maximum Three

Three colors per screen. Three type sizes. Three hierarchy levels. Restraint in palette, boldness in execution.

Geometric Truth

Circles, rectangles, triangles — pure geometry. The notch becomes a bold divider. Concentric circles echo the sun motif from poster references.

Flat & Fearless

No gradients on UI. No drop shadows. Color fields are flat and confident. Depth from overlap and layering only.

System III
Grand Tour
“She was dressed in pale blue and her eyes matched the sea at Cap Ferrat”
The Hitchcock direction. Cool blues, pearl whites, noir-ish sophistication — the Côte d’Azur at twilight, Grace Kelly in the passenger seat, Cary Grant at the wheel. Composed and mysteriously elegant. The palette centers on deep midnight blue and platinum, with the original Royal Blue as throughline. Typography is high-contrast serif — cinematic and deliberate. Like an invitation to a private screening at Cannes.

Design Principles

Cinematic Restraint

Less is more, but what remains must be exquisite. Every element feels placed by a film’s art director — intentional, atmospheric, never busy.

Night-First

Dark mode is the primary canvas. Deep midnight blue creates drama and makes Royal Blue and Champagne luminous. Light mode feels like stepping from a cinema into daylight.

Literary Voice

Micro-copy reads like prose. Error messages are gracious. Loading states suggest anticipation. The app should feel like turning pages of a beautiful novel about your next trip.

Whispered Luxury

No flashiness, no “premium” badges. Luxury via spacing, restraint, quality of interaction. The notch as a barely-there separator — always felt, rarely seen.

A Note on Direction
These three systems aren’t mutually exclusive.
The strongest path forward might be to pick one as primary and selectively borrow from the others. Grand Tour’s dark-mode sophistication with Riviera d’Oro’s warm accents. Or Manifesto’s typographic boldness tempered by Grand Tour’s cinematic restraint. The Vialoure brand is strong enough to hold all three — the question is which emotional frequency you want travelers to feel when they open the app.
